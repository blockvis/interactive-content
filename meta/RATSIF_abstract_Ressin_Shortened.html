TECHNICAL DEBT & THE BEAUTY OF COMPLEX-VALUED GEOMETRY AND ARITHMETIC

Anatoly Ressin

Blockvis SIA, BlockvisLabs

Latgales iela 240-3, room 504, Riga, LV-1063, Latvia

anatoly@blockvis.com

Keywords: History and Technical Debt of Mathematics, Eisenstein Integers, Gosper Island, Positional Coding, GQ128 Format, AI Visualization

In 1831, Gauss (1831) lamented that complex numbers had been “enveloped in mystery and surrounded by darkness” — and blamed not the mathematics but the words. Had +1, −1, and √−1 been named direct, inverse, and lateral rather than positive, negative, and imaginary, the darkness would never have formed. The notation descended from Euler (1748), whose Introductio cemented conventions modern students still inherit. This talk takes Gauss’s complaint further than he did — through the axes, the matrix layout, the lattice, and the unit interval — arguing that each layer of the standard formalism carries a technical debt whose repayment reveals unexpected beauty.

The complex plane, first given geometric form by the Norwegian surveyor Wessel (1799), was born in a navigational frame where forward pointed up and lateral pointed sideways. In this orientation the positive rotation from Re to Im runs clockwise — matching a compass and a clock. The Argand diagram inherited the Cartesian convention instead: Re horizontal, counter-clockwise positive rotation. Restoring Wessel’s frame makes eiθ turn like a clock hand and recovers Gauss’s direct and lateral units. A second debt hides in matrix notation: the standard 2×2 layout of a Hermitian matrix H = H† presents its symmetry as an algebraic constraint. Standing the matrix on its corner as a diamond turns conjugate-transpose into a visible geometric reflection — the off-diagonal entries z and ̅z become literal mirror images across the vertical diagonal.

These are representational debts. The deeper one is structural. Gauss’s treatise also introduced the Gaussian integers ℤ[i], encoding the 90° convention as a square lattice. Yet the square lattice is not optimal: the hexagonal lattice is denser, with six equidistant neighbours per point rather than four. The Eisenstein integers ℤ[ω], where ω = e2πi/3 satisfies ω² + ω + 1 = 0, form precisely this hexagonal lattice (Eisenstein, 1844; Ireland and Rosen, 1990). Their norm N(a + bω) = a² − ab + b² is the quadratic form of 60° geometry, and they constitute a Euclidean domain. The hexagonal lattice is not a generalisation; it is a correction.

Knuth (1960) first observed that a complex number could serve as the base of a positional numeral system. The Eisenstein setting yields the most elegant instance: choosing β = 2 − ω gives N(β) = 4 − 2 + 1 = 7, so there are exactly seven residue classes and the digit set is D = {0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}. Every Eisenstein integer then admits a unique finite representation:

z = dn · βn + dn−1 · βn−1 + ··· + d0,   dk ∈ D     (1)

The analogous “unit interval” — the attractor of all infinite digit expansions over D — is not an interval but a fractal: the Gosper island, built from seven self-similar copies at factor 1/√7, first described by Gardner (1976) and named by Mandelbrot (1977). It tiles the plane by translation alone, with no gaps and a boundary nowhere smooth.

These observations converge at a practical consequence. The talk introduces GQ128 — a 128-bit complex floating-point format whose mantissa is an Eisenstein integer rather than an ordinary integer. Just as the Setun ternary computer (Brusentsov and Ramil Alvarez, 2011) exploited balanced ternary to achieve more efficient real arithmetic, GQ128 exploits the hexagonal lattice of ℤ[ω] to achieve more efficient complex arithmetic: its quantization error is isotropic — uniform in all directions in the complex plane — a property structurally impossible on the square lattice underlying IEEE 754. All concepts, from the navigational axis swap through the Gosper island to GQ128, are demonstrated through interactive visualizations generated in real time by AI, with no conventional slides.
